# Goalie Valuation — The Most Volatile Position

Goalies are the hardest position to evaluate in hockey. Their performance swings wildly year-to-year, they develop later than skaters, and the market consistently overpays for them. Here's what our data shows.

## How We Measure Goalies

Our goalie metric is **Goals Saved Above Expected (GSAx)**, converted to WAR:

1. For every shot faced, calculate the expected goal probability (xGoal from MoneyPuck)
2. Sum up expected goals against (xGA) across all shots
3. **GSAx = xGA - Actual Goals Against** (positive = saved more than expected)
4. Adjust for league-average to remove seasonal xG model bias
5. Set replacement level at the 25th percentile of starters (1,000+ shots)
6. **Goalie WAR = Goals Above Replacement / 6.0**

## Why Goalies Are Different

**Later development:** Skaters peak at 25-28. Goalies peak at 27-30. A goalie drafted at 18 typically needs 4-6 years of development before contributing at the NHL level, compared to 1-3 years for a top-6 forward or top-4 defenseman.

**Higher variance:** Goalie WAR has much higher year-to-year variance than skater WAR. A goalie who posts 5.0 WAR one season might post 1.0 the next. This makes long-term goalie contracts inherently riskier.

**Lower draft hit rate:** Only ~10% of drafted goalies become NHL starters. Compare to ~25% of first-round skaters becoming top-6 forwards or top-4 defensemen.

## Draft Strategy Implications

Given the late development, high variance, and low hit rate, our data suggests:

- **Don't spend premium draft capital on goalies.** The expected surplus from a goalie pick is lower than an equivalent skater pick in every round.
- **Trade for established goalies** rather than drafting them. The uncertainty discount on goalie development is massive.
- **Short-term goalie contracts** outperform long-term ones because of the high variance. A 2-3 year deal lets you re-evaluate, while a 7-year deal locks you in through inevitable decline.

## Positional Value Comparison

When we compare WAR distributions across positions:

- **Forwards** produce the most total WAR (more roster spots, higher offensive ceiling)
- **Defensemen** are the most stable year-to-year (defensive metrics are more repeatable)
- **Goalies** have the highest peak WAR (a hot goalie can single-handedly carry a team) but also the highest variance

This creates an interesting strategic tension: goalies have the highest potential impact but the lowest predictability, making them the riskiest investment at every level — draft, trade, and free agency.
